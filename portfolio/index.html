Research Identity

Vedānta
& Mind

Advaita Vedānta is not a religious system. It is the most rigorous philosophy of consciousness ever written. The question it asks — what is the nature of the witness, the knower, pure awareness — is the same question that David Chalmers calls the hard problem of consciousness. One tradition is 3,000 years old. The other is thirty.

I am a Sanskrit scholar and Vedāntin first. Computation came later, as a method — a way to make these philosophical systems formally accessible, analyzable, and legible to modern researchers who do not read Sanskrit. The Brahmasūtrabhāṣya, the Upaniṣads, Bhartṛhari’s Sphoṭa theory, the Vachana corpus — these are not data sources to me. They are philosophical arguments I have lived with.

What interests me most is the intersection: where Ādi Śaṅkarā’s account of Adhyāsa (superimposition) meets contemporary theories of perception and misrepresentation. Where the Antaḥkaraṇa model of mind — manas, buddhi, ahaṃkāra, citta — maps onto (and sometimes corrects) modern cognitive architecture. Where Śabda as pramāṇa gives language a epistemic weight that Western philosophy of language is only beginning to consider.

प्रज्ञानं ब्रह्म — Consciousness itself is the ground of all inquiry.

🗣️
Language & Mind
Sphoṭa Theory & Philosophy of Language
Bhartṛhari’s Sphoṭa theory holds that language and consciousness are not separate — that the meaning of a sentence is grasped as a whole, in an instant of cognition called Pratibhā. Mīmāṃsā treats Śabda (sound/word) as an independent means of valid knowledge. These are radical philosophical positions. They also happen to be directly relevant to how large language models fail to understand meaning.
वाक्यं रसवदास्वाद्यं — Speech is the taste of all things. Bhartṛhari
🧠
Modern Bridge
Computation as Philosophical Method
The tools I build — RAG systems over the Brahmasūtrabhāṣya, semantic search for Sanskrit ślokas, LLM pipelines for Vachana poetry — are not ends in themselves. They are how I make these philosophical systems legible to researchers who do not read Sanskrit. The goal is always philosophical clarity. The method is sometimes computational.
